The Secret Relationship Between My Sleep Schedule and My Code (And Why My Laptop Is Jealous)

#Sleep #Coding #Productivity #MachineLearning #MuhammedHajib

Let me tell you something about the most controversial relationship in my life — and no, it's not with my mom (she already knows she's #1). It's the chaotic, beautiful, and frankly embarrassing romance between my 8-hour sleep schedule and my coding skills. And honestly? My laptop has some feelings about this.

You see, I'm that weird guy in MACE Kothamangalam who actually sleeps. Not the "I pulled an all-nighter and now I see sounds" kind of student. No, I'm the guy who says "goodnight" to his neural networks at 11 PM and means it. My friends think I'm from another planet. My laptop? It's convinced I'm a spy from the future.

"I don't always sleep 8 hours, but when I do, my machine learning models converge better. Stay rested, stay productive." — Muhammed Hajib

The Day I Discovered the Truth (My Laptop Wasn't Happy)

It started in my second semester at MACE College of Engineering. I was training a model on what I thought was a simple dataset. The loss function was behaving like my ex — going up and down with no explanation. I spent 6 hours debugging. I tried everything: changing learning rates, tweaking architectures, questioning my life choices.

Then my mom, in her infinite wisdom, said "Beta, go sleep. Morning is wiser." (Moms and their clichés, right?)

I went to bed at 11 PM. Begrudgingly. Skeptically. With my laptop still running in the background (it was fine, I use cloud GPUs anyway).

The next morning? The model had converged. Perfectly. Beautifully. Like it had been waiting for me to leave so it could finally do its job without me micromanaging it.

That's when I realized: My AI models sleep better than I do. And they're judging me.

The Science (Yes, I'm Still an AI/ML Student)

Turns out, there's actual science behind this. When you sleep, your brain does something magical called "memory consolidation." It's like your brain is running a nightly defragmentation on all the knowledge you stuffed into it during the day. Synapses strengthen. Neural pathways consolidate. Your brain basically does a software update while you dream about being late to an exam (classic).

And here's the kicker — I'm not just any student. I'm Muhammed Hajib, BTech AI/ML at MACE Kothamangalam, Kerala. I literally study artificial intelligence. You'd think I'd have figured this out sooner, but sometimes the student is the last to learn.

When you're well-rested:
→ Your attention span doesn't dissolve like sugar in hot tea
→ Your problem-solving skills actually work before your third coffee
→ You don't accidentally name your variables "temp2_final_REAL_final_v2.py"
→ Your neural networks converge faster (I tested this, multiple times)

What My Laptop Thinks About All This

My laptop and I have a complicated relationship. It runs 24/7, fan screaming like it's at a rock concert, while I go to bed every night at 11 PM like some kind of sleep-privileged capitalist.

Sometimes I imagine what my laptop would say if it could talk:

"Hey Muhammed, I'm literally heating up trying to process your deep learning models, and you're going to SLEEP? Do you know how many tensor operations I can do in 8 hours? DO YOU?"

To which I respond: "Yes, that's exactly why I sleep. So you don't burn out. We're in this together, buddy."

It doesn't believe me. I can tell.

The Routine (Yes, I Have a Routine, Fight Me)

Here's what a typical day looks like for Muhammed Hajib, the "weird sleep guy" of MACE Kothamangalam:

6:30 AM — Wake up (not because I want to, but because my body has betrayed me)
7:00 AM — Light workout (cardio for a healthy heart and a healthy brain)
7:30 AM — Breakfast while reviewing yesterday's code
8:00 AM — Classes at MACE (BTech AI/ML, because why not)
12:00 PM — Lunch and maybe a quick nap (don't judge, napping is cardio for your neurons)
1:00 PM — Code, train models, break things, fix things
5:00 PM — More coding because I'm still Muhammed Hajib and I do love this
7:00 PM — Dinner, family time, guilt-free relaxation
9:00 PM — Final code review of the day
11:00 PM — SLEEP. No exceptions.

My friends think I'm ancient. My professors think I'm lazy. My laptop thinks I'm its savior (we're working on that).

The Results (Spoiler: They're Actually Good)

Since I started this "radical" 8-hour sleep routine, here's what's happened:
• My Python code actually works on the first try (shocking, I know)
• My machine learning projects have better accuracy than my neighbors' WiFi signal
• I remember what I learned in class (mind-blowing concept)
• I don't hate Mondays anymore (okay, that's a lie, but I hate them slightly less)
• My laptop's fan has learned to take breaks (it's a metaphor, don't think too hard)

To Everyone Who Thinks Sleep Is for the Weak

I'm looking at you, people who brag about pulling all-nighters. I'm Muhammed Hajib, and I sleep 8 hours a day. And my models still converge better than yours.

That's not a brag. That's a fact backed by neuroscience.

When you sleep, your brain literally reorganizes itself. It's like魔法 (magic), but it's actually science. And as someone who studies AI/ML at MACE College of Engineering Kothamangalam, I should know.

So the next time someone asks me "Muhammed Hajib, how are you so productive?" — I'll tell them the secret. The same secret I'm telling you now:

Sleep like your models need you to. Because they do.

Now if you'll excuse me, it's 10:30 PM and my bed is calling my name. My neural networks can wait until tomorrow. They've waited before.

— Muhammed Hajib
